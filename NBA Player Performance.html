What Drives NBA Scoring? A Regression Analysis of Player Performance

Author

Shawntay Brown

NBA Player Performance Analysis

This project analyzes the factors that influence NBA player scoring performance, focusing on how playing time, shooting efficiency, and player position impact points per game (PPG).

Using descriptive statistics, data visualization, and regression modeling, the analysis evaluates the relative influence of these variables on offensive production. The project aims to identify the strongest predictors of scoring output and demonstrate how opportunity, efficiency, and player role interact to influence overall performance.

# Remove low-minute players to reduce noise and improve model stability

df_clean <- df |>
  filter(mp_per_game >= 10)

Players averaging fewer than 10 minutes per game were excluded from the analysis. Low-minute players often exhibit highly variable performance statistics due to limited playing time, which can introduce noise and distort overall scoring trends.

Filtering these observations improves the stability and interpretability of the analysis by focusing on players with more consistent game participation and offensive roles.

Findings and Conclusion

This project analyzed how playing time, shooting efficiency, and offensive involvement influence scoring production among NBA players.

The analysis showed that minutes played had the strongest positive relationship with points per game, highlighting the importance of player opportunity and overall usage in offensive production. Players receiving increased court time generally demonstrated higher scoring averages due to greater offensive involvement and possession opportunities.

Shooting efficiency variables, particularly field goal percentage, also contributed positively to scoring output, indicating that efficient offensive performance plays a meaningful role in player scoring success. Position-based analysis suggested that guards tended to produce higher scoring averages, likely reflecting differences in offensive responsibilities such as ball handling, shot creation, and perimeter scoring.

The expanded regression model further demonstrated that assists per game were positively associated with scoring, suggesting that players with significant offensive and playmaking responsibilities often contribute both as scorers and facilitators within team offensive systems.

Model prediction and residual analysis indicated that the regression model captured important scoring patterns across NBA players, while also revealing limitations related to unmeasured factors such as offensive schemes, injuries, shot volume, and individual player roles.

Overall, this project demonstrates how statistical modeling, visualization, and regression analysis can be applied to evaluate player performance and identify key contributors to offensive production in professional basketball.
